This is what happened next.

Chapter One

Lost

At eighteen, I dropped out of EPI.

I didn't have a plan. I didn't have a direction. I just knew the path I was on wasn't the right one.

One of my teachers told me I might not amount to anything. Those words didn't roll off my back the way people say they should. They stuck. They burrowed in. For the next two years, they became a kind of prophecy I was living into without even realizing it.

My father wanted me to come work at his retail shop. He had his way of doing things — his systems, his methods, his rules. I respected that, but I couldn't just slot myself into someone else's vision. Not even my father's. Something in me resisted it. I didn't know what I wanted. I just knew it wasn't that.

So between eighteen and twenty, I was adrift. I played too many video games. I stayed up too late. I woke up too late. I watched days blur into each other. The kind of lost where you're not even looking for the exit because you don't realize you're in a maze.

But underneath the surface, something was building. A restlessness. A quiet anger at myself. A growing awareness that if I didn't do something — anything — the teacher might be right.

"I didn't know what I wanted to become. I just knew I couldn't stay where I was."

Chapter Two

The First Move

At twenty, I decided I had to do something. I didn't have a business plan. I didn't have mentors. I didn't have capital. What I had was the internet, Alibaba, and a burning need to prove — mostly to myself — that I could build something.

I saw an opportunity in sourcing products from China. Aruba is a small island with a massive tourism economy, and souvenir shops were everywhere. If I could find the right product at the right price and get it in front of the right people, maybe I could make something work.

There was one problem: I had no money. Zero.

My current wife's mother believed in me enough to give me a loan. That's a weight most people don't understand. It wasn't just capital — it was trust. Someone else's hard-earned money placed in the hands of a twenty-year-old dropout with a half-formed idea and a lot of ambition. I wasn't going to waste it.

I placed my first order on Alibaba. When the product arrived, it was real. Tangible. Something I could hold and sell. For the first time in two years, I felt like I was moving forward.

Now I had to sell it.

Chapter Three

Door to Door

My wife and I went door to door. Every souvenir shop on the island. We walked in with samples, introduced ourselves, and pitched.

Most of them said no.

Some were polite about it. Some weren't. Some didn't even let us finish. That was the first time I felt real rejection — not the kind where someone ignores your text or doesn't show up. The kind where you're standing in front of someone, putting yourself out there, and they look at you and say no. To your face. While you're holding the product you spent your last money on.

I was angry. I was frustrated. Part of me wanted to quit. The doubts came flooding in — maybe the teacher was right, maybe I wasn't cut out for this, maybe I should just go work at my dad's shop and stop pretending.

But I didn't quit.

Instead, I made a decision that would define everything that came after: I chose to treat rejection as information, not as a verdict. Every "no" was data. It told me something about my pitch, my product, my timing, my presentation. It wasn't personal. It was preparation for the next attempt.

"Rejection didn't break me. It taught me that every 'no' just means you show up better prepared for the next one."

We managed to get some customers. Not many, but enough. Enough to prove that the model worked. Enough to reinvest the capital into a second product. And then a third. Each time, a little better. A little sharper. A little more confident.

Something else happened during this time: my curiosity caught fire. I started studying. Reading. Learning about business, about supply chains, about margins, about sales psychology. The passion for learning that had been dormant during those lost years exploded. Every problem became a puzzle I wanted to solve. Every obstacle became a system I wanted to build.

Chapter Four

The Stranger in My Father's Store

One day, about a year into my wholesaling hustle, I went to visit my dad at his store. I wasn't there for business — just stopping by, maybe five minutes before I had to leave.

That's when I noticed a man walking through the store. He was looking at the products, but not the way a customer does. He wasn't checking prices. He was reading labels. Looking at where things were made. Studying the brands.

I knew instantly: this wasn't a customer. This was a supplier. Or a buyer. Or someone in the business.

I can't explain what happened next except to call it a gut sensation. A feeling. His energy was different. There was something about the way he carried himself, the way he studied the merchandise, that told me this was a moment I shouldn't let pass. To this day, I don't fully understand it. But I've learned to trust it.

I walked up to him and asked what he needed.

He told me he was opening a store right across the street from my father's shop. He was looking to expand his business and was scouting the market. I told him I had started wholesaling about a year ago and that I'd just brought in my second product from China.

That was music to his ears.

Right there, on the spot, he asked if I wanted to partner with him.

I didn't believe it. My first instinct was skepticism. This felt like a scam. Too good to be true. A stranger walks into your father's store and offers you a partnership? That doesn't happen to twenty-one-year-old dropouts from Aruba.

We exchanged numbers. I was excited but scared. The overthinking started immediately — what if it's a con? What if he disappears? What if I'm being naive?

I messaged him. No response. Messaged again. Nothing. Days turned into a week. A week turned into two. I kept sending messages, trying not to sound desperate, trying to stay professional, but inside I was spiraling. I'd already started building this up in my head and now it was falling apart.

Three weeks later, he replied.

He'd been on vacation. That's it. Just on vacation. He told me he had merchandise ready to be sent out.

$90,000 in merchandise.

I couldn't believe it. When my freight forwarder sent me the arrival confirmation for the goods, I sat there staring at the notification. This was more product than I'd handled in my entire life. This wasn't just a deal — it was a catapult.

"What I learned is this: if you are actively doing something — executing, going out there, showing up — you create the conditions for luck to find you. These moments don't present themselves to people sitting at home. They present themselves to people who are already in motion."

That partnership put me on the map in the industry. People knew my name. I had credibility. And with credibility came the next opportunity.

Chapter Five

The First Acquisition

The $90K deal opened doors I didn't even know existed. One of those doors led to the opportunity to acquire my first retail store.

The asking price was more money than we had. Between inventory, assets, and receivables, our cash was tied up. We couldn't just write a check — we had suppliers to pay, operations to fund, commitments to honor.

But my wife and I looked at each other and said: we're doing this.

We agreed to the purchase without knowing exactly how we were going to pay for it. That might sound reckless. But we'd built a pattern by then — a pattern of figuring it out. Of finding a way when there didn't seem to be one. That's what we do. That's what we've always done.

We went to friends and family for loans. Not my parents — they weren't on board with the idea. They couldn't see what we saw. So we went elsewhere. We pieced it together. We got the money. We paid the seller.

And we opened the next week.

Chapter Six

$68

I still remember our first day. We opened the doors at 9 AM, bright and eager, full of that nervous energy that comes with betting everything on something.

We sold $68 the entire day. Nine in the morning to nine at night. Twelve hours. Sixty-eight dollars.

The panic set in immediately. We had debt from the acquisition. Loans from friends and family. A rent contract. Supplier obligations. And we just did sixty-eight dollars on day one. The math didn't add up. The math was terrifying.

Day two: $96.

The doubt was crushing. I started thinking the seller had manipulated the numbers. That we'd been played. That I'd dragged my wife, my family, our entire financial future into a hole we couldn't climb out of.

Day three: over $500.

The relief was physical. I felt it in my chest. Something shifted that day — not just in the numbers, but in my understanding of business. Revenue isn't linear. Customers don't arrive on a schedule. The early days of anything are chaos, and you have to survive the chaos long enough for the patterns to emerge.

"$68 on day one nearly broke us. Not financially — mentally. But we held on. And the rest is history."

We've had that location for eight years now.

Chapter Seven

What I'm Building Now

From that first store, we kept building. One became two. Two became four. Along the way we launched Nidalas Print Co. — a B2B screen printing operation. We created LAMA, an apparel brand rooted in Aruban culture. We started Proteus 8, a prefab housing venture aimed at the Dutch Caribbean.

All of it still runs under one roof — Nidalas Enterprises — and all of it still runs with the same two people who went door to door a decade ago. My wife and I. Same partnership. Same argument at the kitchen table about which risk to take next.

What's changed is how we build.

I'm a systems thinker. I see the end state of something before the first step is taken, then I work backward. For most of my twenties, that meant whiteboards and spreadsheets and too many late nights translating vision into instructions a team could execute. Now it means AI. Automation. Agents that handle the work that used to eat my weeks. I'm not romantic about the tools — I use whatever lets a small-island operation punch above its weight in a global economy. Today that happens to be AI. Ten years ago it was Alibaba. The instinct is the same. Find the lever nobody in your market is pulling yet, and pull it before they figure out it exists.

Right now, that means building CJS — a retail concept I'm structuring to run leaner than anything I've operated before, with automation handling the work that used to require five people. It means bringing prefab housing to an island that desperately needs it, at a price point local construction can't match. It means LAMA scaling beyond Aruba without losing the thing that makes it Aruban.

I'm still getting told no. Different rooms, different stakes, but the same word. The difference is I don't flinch anymore. Somewhere between the souvenir shops and now, rejection stopped being something that happened to me and became something I use. Data. Signal. A map of where to push next.

The teacher who told me I wouldn't amount to anything was wrong about the outcome. But he was right about one thing — the path I was on at eighteen would not have led anywhere good. What he couldn't see was that the path I needed wasn't going to be handed to me. I was going to have to build it.

I'm still building it.

— Christian Saladin, Aruba, 2026
